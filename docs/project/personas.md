# Personas

## Primary personas

### 1. Guided Outfit Shopper

**Who they are**

A customer browsing one product category, usually online, who wants help turning a single item into a complete outfit.

**Goals**

- understand which complementary items go together
- build confidence in style choices quickly
- discover accessories or layering pieces they may not have considered

**Pain points**

- too many choices without enough styling guidance
- existing recommendations feel generic or repetitive
- uncertainty about matching colors, fabrics, and formality levels

**Typical behaviors**

- enters from search, category pages, campaigns, or a PDP
- browses a small number of anchor products such as a suit or jacket
- compares items visually and is influenced by complete looks

**Decision context**

- often shopping in-session with limited patience
- influenced by occasion, season, climate, and budget
- may purchase immediately if the look feels coherent and complete

**Success for this persona**

They can move from an anchor product to a credible full outfit with minimal uncertainty and higher purchase confidence.

### 2. Returning Style-Aware Customer

**Who they are**

A repeat customer with prior purchases, browsing history, or loyalty signals that can inform more personal recommendations.

**Goals**

- receive recommendations that fit their existing wardrobe and preferences
- avoid duplicate or irrelevant suggestions
- discover new combinations that still feel aligned with their taste

**Pain points**

- generic recommendations ignore what they already own
- existing systems rarely reflect evolving preferences or recent purchases
- recommendations can feel disconnected across email, site, and store experiences

**Typical behaviors**

- arrives from email, remarketing, saved products, or direct return visits
- alternates between inspiration and specific replenishment or occasion-driven missions
- may engage across multiple sessions and channels before buying

**Decision context**

- higher expectations for relevance because the brand already knows them
- expects continuity across devices and channels
- may be more open to premium or higher-basket recommendations when trust is high

**Success for this persona**

Recommendations feel aware of their style history, current intent, and wardrobe gaps rather than treating them like a first-time shopper.

### 3. Occasion-Driven Shopper

**Who they are**

A customer shopping for a defined event or context such as a wedding, interview, business travel, or seasonal wardrobe change.

**Goals**

- assemble an outfit appropriate for the occasion
- understand recommended formality, seasonality, and supporting pieces
- reduce the effort of translating an event into product choices

**Pain points**

- occasion requirements are not always obvious from single-item browsing
- customers may not know whether they need accessories, outerwear, or CM upgrades
- relevance changes by location, climate, and event timing

**Typical behaviors**

- may start from search, inspiration content, or marketing campaigns
- often explores multiple categories in one journey
- may compare different outfit directions before purchasing

**Decision context**

- time-sensitive and mission-oriented
- more likely to respond to structured outfit bundles or occasion-based recommendations
- needs recommendations that balance aspiration with practicality

**Success for this persona**

They receive a clear outfit direction for the occasion, with recommended items that fit context, climate, and brand style.

## Secondary personas

### 4. In-Store Stylist / Clienteling Associate

**Goals**

- use recommendations to support conversations and appointments
- assemble outfits faster without sacrificing brand credibility
- personalize suggestions using client history and current needs

**Pain points**

- customer history may be fragmented across systems
- building looks manually takes time and depends on individual expertise
- cross-channel recommendations are often inconsistent with store reality

**Success**

They can use recommendation outputs as a trusted assistive tool, not as a black box they need to override constantly.

### 5. Merchandiser / Look Curator

**Goals**

- encode brand styling logic, campaigns, and curated looks into the platform
- preserve control over assortments, rules, exclusions, and strategic emphasis
- understand how curated and AI-ranked recommendations perform

**Pain points**

- manual curation does not scale to every context and customer
- merchandising rules are often spread across teams or systems
- weak observability makes it hard to tune recommendation behavior

**Success**

They can steer recommendation outcomes through curated looks, rules, and governance while still benefiting from automation and personalization.

### 6. Marketing / Optimization Team

**Goals**

- use recommendation outputs in email and personalization campaigns
- test variants and measure business impact
- segment customers based on profile, behavior, and intent

**Pain points**

- channel-specific recommendation logic is hard to compare and improve
- experiment setup is inconsistent when data and delivery are fragmented
- low traceability makes attribution difficult

**Success**

They can target audiences and campaigns with recommendation sets that are measurable, reusable, and commercially meaningful.

### 7. Product / Analytics Team

**Goals**

- improve recommendation quality through experimentation and analysis
- understand performance by surface, cohort, source, and context
- maintain reliable platform capabilities for future channel expansion

**Pain points**

- scattered logic makes experimentation slow
- event data may not connect recommendation exposure to outcomes cleanly
- gaps in data quality or identity resolution can distort results

**Success**

They can treat recommendation decisioning as a measurable product capability with clear inputs, outputs, and performance signals.
