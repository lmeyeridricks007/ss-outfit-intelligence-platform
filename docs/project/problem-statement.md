# Problem Statement

## Current problem

SuitSupply customers often shop for tailoring, shirts, shoes, and accessories as part of a complete outfit, but most ecommerce recommendation patterns treat products as isolated items. This leaves customers to manually figure out how to combine garments, accessories, and seasonal considerations into a coherent look.

Typical customer questions include:

- what shirt matches this suit?
- which tie works with this jacket?
- what should I wear for a specific occasion?
- how do I complete this purchase into a full outfit?
- what fits my style history rather than generic popularity?

## Who experiences the problem

The problem affects several groups:

### Primary customer groups

- online shoppers browsing RTW products across multiple categories
- returning customers whose prior purchases and style preferences are not fully reflected in recommendations
- customers shopping for occasions such as weddings, business meetings, interviews, travel, or seasonal wardrobe needs
- CM customers who need coordinated guidance while configuring garments and accessories

### Internal groups

- in-store stylists who need quick, relevant outfit suggestions
- merchandisers who want controlled influence over recommendation outcomes
- marketing teams that need personalized recommendation content for campaigns
- analytics and product teams responsible for proving recommendation value

## Why current alternatives are insufficient

Most conventional recommendation systems emphasize:

- similar items
- frequently bought together patterns
- popularity-based ranking
- limited rules that do not reflect full outfit compatibility

These approaches are insufficient because they do not reliably account for:

- style compatibility across categories
- occasion and formality
- weather, country, and season
- customer profile and purchase history
- RTW versus CM recommendation differences
- merchandising intent, curated looks, and business constraints

As a result, current alternatives can surface plausible adjacent products without actually solving the customer's outfit-building problem.

## Why now

The opportunity is timely because SuitSupply already has multiple inputs needed to make outfit intelligence valuable:

- strong product and styling expertise
- multiple digital and in-store recommendation surfaces
- growing volumes of customer behavior and transaction data
- marketing and clienteling use cases that benefit from better personalization

Without a platform approach, these signals remain fragmented and the business continues to underuse its styling knowledge.

## Consequences of not solving it

If SuitSupply does not address this problem:

- customers will continue to experience friction when trying to complete outfits
- conversion and average order value gains from cross-category styling will remain unrealized
- recommendation quality will stay inconsistent across channels
- internal teams will rely on manual or fragmented workflows that do not scale
- competitors using more advanced personalization may narrow the differentiation gap

## Core problem framing

SuitSupply needs a recommendation platform that can combine curated styling knowledge, compatibility rules, customer behavior, and real-world context to deliver complete, explainable outfit recommendations across channels.
