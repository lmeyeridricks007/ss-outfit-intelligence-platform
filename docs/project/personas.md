# Personas

## Persona 1: Occasion-led online shopper

### Who they are

A customer shopping for a specific event such as a wedding, interview, business trip, or seasonal wardrobe update. They may know the dress code they need to satisfy, but not which combination of products creates the right outfit.

### Goals

- find a complete outfit that fits the occasion
- avoid mismatched combinations or inappropriate styling choices
- reduce time spent comparing individual products across categories

### Pain points

- uncertainty about what items work together
- difficulty translating occasion needs into specific products
- too many choices with little confident guidance

### Behaviors

- browses multiple categories in one session
- evaluates products through imagery and occasion cues
- is likely to respond to outfit examples and ready-made bundles

### Decision-making context

This customer often has moderate urgency, a defined end use, and limited tolerance for recommendation noise. Context such as season, weather, and destination matters.

### Success looks like

They quickly find a credible complete outfit and add multiple compatible items to cart in one shopping journey.

## Persona 2: Returning style-aware customer

### Who they are

A repeat SuitSupply customer with prior purchases, browsing history, and emerging style preferences. They expect recommendations to reflect what they already own, what they tend to buy, and what fits their personal aesthetic.

### Goals

- receive recommendations that feel personally relevant
- discover new complementary items without duplicating owned wardrobe pieces
- extend prior purchases into new looks or seasonal updates

### Pain points

- generic recommendations that ignore purchase history
- repeated suggestions for items they already own or do not need
- limited recognition of style preferences, fit preferences, or price tolerance

### Behaviors

- returns across multiple sessions and channels
- reacts positively to personalized suggestions that feel curated
- may engage with email, saved items, wishlists, or account history

### Decision-making context

This customer expects personalization quality to improve over time. Trust matters: irrelevant suggestions degrade confidence quickly.

### Success looks like

They experience the platform as an intelligent personal style assistant and buy additional categories beyond the original product of interest.

## Persona 3: RTW and CM style explorer

### Who they are

A customer exploring both ready-made products and more tailored or premium options. They may configure a CM garment while needing guidance on shirts, ties, fabrics, color palettes, and finishing choices that work together.

### Goals

- understand compatible choices while configuring a garment
- balance personal taste with stylistically sound combinations
- discover premium add-ons that improve the final outfit

### Pain points

- uncertainty about fabric, color, and accessory coordination
- lack of guidance that adapts as configuration choices change
- recommendations that treat CM like standard product similarity

### Behaviors

- spends longer within product detail and configuration flows
- compares multiple variants before committing
- values confidence-building explanations and polished recommendations

### Decision-making context

Decision complexity is higher than typical catalog browsing, and recommendation logic must respond to in-progress configuration choices.

### Success looks like

The customer completes a configured garment plus compatible complementary items with confidence in the final look.

## Secondary persona 1: In-store stylist or clienteling associate

### Goals

- assemble relevant outfits quickly for appointments or follow-up outreach
- use customer history and occasion context to guide recommendations
- maintain professional styling quality without manual catalog scanning

### Pain points

- fragmented customer context across systems
- time lost building looks manually
- limited visibility into which recommendations are curated, rule-based, or AI-ranked

### Success looks like

The stylist can use the platform to support higher-quality appointments, faster follow-up, and stronger outfit completion.

## Secondary persona 2: Merchandiser or style curator

### Goals

- define curated looks, seasonal priorities, and business rules
- influence recommendation outputs without engineering dependency for every change
- balance brand presentation with performance goals

### Pain points

- limited control over automated recommendation logic
- difficulty auditing why a recommendation was shown
- manual campaign and curation work that does not scale

### Success looks like

The merchandiser can publish, monitor, and refine curated looks and rules with clear visibility into performance and override behavior.

## Secondary persona 3: Marketing and growth operator

### Goals

- activate personalized outfit recommendations in lifecycle and campaign communications
- improve engagement and purchase behavior from recommendation-driven messages
- target customers by style, occasion, and affinity signals

### Pain points

- inconsistent recommendation payloads across channels
- slow activation of campaign-specific logic
- weak measurement of recommendation impact

### Success looks like

The marketing team can reuse platform recommendation outputs in campaigns with traceable performance and minimal custom integration work.

## Secondary persona 4: Product, analytics, and optimization lead

### Goals

- measure recommendation quality and business impact
- run experiments safely across surfaces and segments
- understand data coverage, model behavior, and operational gaps

### Pain points

- poor traceability between recommendation logic and outcomes
- siloed measurement by channel
- limited ability to compare curated, rule-based, and AI-ranked strategies

### Success looks like

The team can evaluate recommendation effectiveness using consistent metrics, event data, and experiment design across the platform.
