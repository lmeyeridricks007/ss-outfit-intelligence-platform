# Problem Statement

## Current problem
SuitSupply customers often know the outcome they want, such as a wedding look, interview outfit, or seasonal wardrobe update, but struggle to convert that intent into a complete set of compatible products. They can find a suit, jacket, or shirt, yet still hesitate because they do not know what matches, what is appropriate for the occasion, or what complements prior purchases.

## Who experiences the problem
- Online shoppers browsing tailoring, shirts, shoes, and accessories.
- Returning customers whose prior purchases should influence recommendations.
- Customers shopping for a specific occasion or climate-sensitive need.
- In-store stylists who need fast starting points for clienteling.
- Merchandisers and marketers who need consistent recommendation logic across channels.

## Why current alternatives are insufficient
Most ecommerce recommendation systems emphasize:
- similar items
- frequently bought together bundles
- popularity or co-occurrence signals

Those approaches are insufficient for SuitSupply because they do not reliably model:
- style compatibility across categories
- occasion appropriateness
- weather and seasonal context
- differences between RTW and CM flows
- personal history, wardrobe context, and style preferences
- business controls needed by merchandising and marketing teams

## Why now
The need is urgent because SuitSupply already has the ingredients required for a stronger system:
- rich catalog attributes across tailoring and accessories
- brand styling expertise and curated merchandising knowledge
- multiple channels that would benefit from consistent recommendation logic
- customer data sources that can support more personalized decisioning

Without a platform layer, that knowledge remains fragmented and difficult to scale. As channel count and personalization expectations grow, manual curation alone becomes less consistent, less measurable, and less reusable.

## Consequences of not solving it
If SuitSupply does not solve this problem:
- customers will continue to abandon or limit baskets because they lack styling confidence
- recommendation experiences will remain narrow and less differentiated from competitors
- channel teams will keep creating disconnected recommendation logic and campaign workarounds
- internal curation knowledge will stay difficult to reuse, measure, and improve
- RTW and CM journeys will miss cross-sell and personalization opportunities that require complete-look reasoning
