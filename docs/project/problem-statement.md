# Problem Statement

## Current problem

SuitSupply customers often shop with outfit intent but encounter a journey optimized for individual products. A customer may start from a suit, jacket, shirt, or occasion, yet the harder decision is how to complete the look:

- which shirt, tie, shoes, and belt work together
- what is appropriate for a wedding, interview, business meeting, travel plan, or seasonal need
- which products fit the current weather, season, and location
- how to extend an existing wardrobe without repeating owned items or clashing with prior purchases

Without strong outfit guidance, customers must manually infer compatibility across categories, price points, and style conventions. That friction shows up precisely when a shopper is deciding whether to commit to a basket.

## Who experiences it

### Primary affected users

- online shoppers browsing suits, shirts, shoes, and accessories
- returning customers whose purchase history and style preferences are not reflected consistently
- customers shopping with explicit or implied occasion intent

### Secondary affected users

- in-store stylists and clienteling teams who need faster, more consistent look assembly
- merchandisers who want curated looks, rules, and campaign priorities to scale across channels
- marketing teams that need more relevant recommendation content for lifecycle campaigns
- product, analytics, and optimization teams responsible for improving recommendation performance

## Why current alternatives are insufficient

Typical ecommerce recommendation systems over-index on:

- similar products
- frequently bought together combinations
- top sellers and popularity ranking
- co-occurrence signals without style intelligence

These approaches fail to reliably account for:

- style compatibility across categories
- outfit completeness instead of item adjacency
- occasion and dress-code suitability
- season, weather, geography, and session context
- customer profile, previous purchases, and inferred preferences
- curated brand styling intent and merchandising rules

The result is a recommendation experience that may be mathematically relevant but not stylistically useful.

## Why now

SuitSupply already has the brand authority, product breadth, and internal styling knowledge needed to support complete-look recommendations. What is missing is a shared, governed platform to activate that knowledge consistently.

This is the right moment because:

- customers increasingly expect personalized and contextual commerce experiences
- structured product data, event pipelines, and AI ranking can now materially improve recommendation quality
- SuitSupply operates across ecommerce, email, and in-store channels that would benefit from shared recommendation logic
- the business upside is directly tied to conversion, basket size, attach rate, and repeat purchase behavior

## Consequence of not solving it

If SuitSupply does not solve this problem:

- customers will continue to abandon outfit decisions or buy incomplete baskets
- recommendation modules will underperform by promoting isolated products instead of coherent looks
- merchandising and stylist knowledge will remain difficult to scale across channels
- marketing and clienteling experiences will stay less relevant than they could be
- the brand will leave measurable revenue and differentiation on the table compared with a style-led recommendation experience
