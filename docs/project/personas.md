# Personas

## Persona model

This product serves both customer-facing recommendation consumers and internal users who shape, review, or act on recommendation outputs.

## Primary personas

### 1. Occasion-led online shopper

#### Profile
- Shops with a near-term purpose such as a wedding, interview, business trip, or seasonal wardrobe update
- Often starts with one anchor item or one occasion rather than a fully formed outfit
- Values confidence, coordination, and speed of decision-making

#### Goals
- Build a complete outfit that fits the occasion
- Avoid style mistakes or incompatible combinations
- See recommendations that feel practical and purchasable

#### Pain points
- Unsure which complementary items match the anchor garment
- Too many disconnected product choices across categories
- Generic recommendation carousels do not explain or reflect occasion relevance

#### Behaviors
- Browses by occasion, season, or anchor item
- Compares a small set of final candidate products
- Responds well to ready-made looks and clear complementary suggestions

#### Decision context
- Often under time pressure
- May have a fixed budget or event deadline
- Needs recommendations that balance style quality with convenience

#### Success for this persona
- They can select an anchor product and quickly add a coherent full outfit.

### 2. Style-aware returning customer

#### Profile
- Has previous purchases, browsing history, or account signals
- Likely has established preferences around fit, color palette, formality, or category mix
- Expects personalization to improve over time

#### Goals
- Receive recommendations aligned with prior taste and existing wardrobe
- Discover complementary and premium options without seeing irrelevant repeats
- Build on prior purchases for new use cases or seasons

#### Pain points
- Receives generic recommendations that ignore known preferences
- Sees products that do not fit prior style behavior or region
- Finds it hard to connect new browsing intent with past purchases

#### Behaviors
- Returns across multiple sessions and channels
- Engages with account history, saved items, and repeat categories
- May browse both RTW and CM depending on use case

#### Decision context
- Expects higher recommendation quality because the brand already knows something about them
- May be more willing to respond to upsell and bundle suggestions if relevance is high

#### Success for this persona
- Recommendations feel distinctly personalized, relevant, and progressively smarter across visits.

### 3. Custom Made customer

#### Profile
- Configures garments or explores premium tailoring options
- Needs support beyond finished product matching, including fabric, styling details, and compatible complementary choices
- Often engages with higher-consideration journeys and possibly human assistance

#### Goals
- Configure garments that work well with intended shirts, ties, shoes, and occasions
- Understand premium options and compatibility trade-offs
- Receive guidance that respects the configuration already in progress

#### Pain points
- Standard RTW logic is not enough for configured garments
- Compatibility depends on fabric, color palette, details, and premium option combinations
- Recommendation logic can become confusing if it ignores the in-progress configuration state

#### Behaviors
- Explores combinations iteratively
- May involve store appointments or stylist interaction
- Benefits from explanation and visual coherence more than from simple product similarity

#### Decision context
- More complex purchase journey with higher emotional and monetary stakes
- Recommendation mistakes can undermine trust quickly

#### Success for this persona
- Recommendations reinforce the configured garment choices and help complete a premium, coherent look.

## Secondary personas

### 4. In-store stylist / clienteling associate

#### Goals
- Quickly access outfit suggestions that are credible and easy to adapt for a live customer conversation
- Use recommendation outputs as a starting point rather than manually building every look from scratch

#### Pain points
- Limited time during appointments or store visits
- Recommendation tools are only useful if they reflect live inventory, customer context, and brand styling rules

#### Success
- The system accelerates styling conversations and improves attachment selling without reducing stylist judgment.

### 5. Merchandiser / look curator

#### Goals
- Define curated looks, compatibility rules, campaigns, and overrides
- Protect brand standards while still allowing AI ranking and personalization

#### Pain points
- Manual curation does not scale across all products, regions, and surfaces
- Black-box recommendation logic is hard to trust or govern

#### Success
- Merchandising teams can shape recommendation behavior through explicit tools, rules, and analytics.

### 6. Marketing and CRM manager

#### Goals
- Use recommendation outputs in personalized email and lifecycle campaigns
- Target customers with complete looks tied to intent, season, or prior purchases

#### Pain points
- Channel-specific recommendation generation is slow and inconsistent
- Poor recommendation quality reduces campaign trust and performance

#### Success
- Recommendation outputs are reusable, measurable, and segment-aware for marketing programs.

### 7. Product, analytics, and optimization lead

#### Goals
- Measure recommendation performance across surfaces and strategies
- Run experiments and understand what drives conversion, attachment, and repeat behavior

#### Pain points
- Recommendation logic is hard to evaluate without consistent telemetry and traceability
- Channel fragmentation makes results difficult to compare

#### Success
- The platform exposes measurable recommendation sets, experiment hooks, and clear business outcomes.
