# Personas

## Primary personas

### 1. Occasion-led online shopper
**Who they are:** A customer shopping for a clear outcome such as a wedding, interview, business trip, or seasonal event.

**Goals**
- Build an outfit that feels correct for the occasion.
- Minimize the risk of choosing mismatched items.
- Purchase the core garment and the right supporting items in one journey.

**Pain points**
- Unsure what shirt, tie, shoe, belt, or outerwear combination works with the selected garment.
- Struggles to translate inspiration into a complete basket.
- Has limited patience for generic "you may also like" suggestions.

**Behaviors**
- Starts from a suit, jacket, or seasonal entry point.
- Browses several categories before committing.
- Responds well to curated looks and occasion framing.

**Decision-making context**
- Usually shopping against a deadline or event.
- Needs confidence more than maximum product breadth.
- Values recommendations that feel intentional and polished.

**Success looks like**
- A complete outfit is recommended with minimal effort.
- The customer understands why the pieces work together.
- The purchase includes the core item plus complementary products.

### 2. Returning style-aware customer
**Who they are:** A repeat shopper with purchase history, saved preferences, or account signals that can inform recommendation decisions.

**Goals**
- Receive recommendations that complement an existing wardrobe.
- Avoid duplicate or incompatible purchases.
- Discover upgrades, seasonal additions, or new looks aligned to personal taste.

**Pain points**
- Existing recommenders ignore what is already owned.
- Similar-item suggestions can feel repetitive or irrelevant.
- Personal style signals are not reflected consistently across sessions and channels.

**Behaviors**
- Logs in, revisits categories, and compares products across sessions.
- Responds to lifecycle emails and personalized home or cart experiences.
- May browse differently by season or occasion.

**Decision-making context**
- Balances familiarity with interest in discovery.
- Expects the brand to remember prior purchases and preferences.
- Is more likely to respond to tailored, context-aware recommendations.

**Success looks like**
- Recommendations acknowledge prior purchases and likely needs.
- The customer sees outfits and additions that feel new but coherent.
- The experience improves over time instead of resetting every visit.

## Secondary personas

### 3. In-store stylist / clienteling associate
**Goals**
- Start with a strong outfit proposal for appointments or assisted selling.
- Use platform output as a flexible recommendation aid rather than a rigid script.

**Pain points**
- Pulling together full outfits quickly can be time-consuming when context is incomplete.
- Omnichannel customer history is often fragmented.

**Behaviors**
- Works from appointments, store visits, customer preferences, and occasion context.
- Needs fast, explainable recommendations with inventory awareness.

**Success looks like**
- The platform accelerates clienteling and improves confidence in recommended looks.

### 4. Merchandiser / style curator
**Goals**
- Define curated looks, compatibility logic, campaign rules, and overrides.
- Maintain brand consistency while allowing AI optimization.

**Pain points**
- Style knowledge is hard to operationalize consistently across channels.
- Purely model-driven recommendations can drift from brand standards.

**Behaviors**
- Curates looks by season, occasion, and product strategy.
- Needs governance, preview, override, and measurement tools.

**Success looks like**
- Curated and AI-ranked recommendations work together under clear controls.

### 5. Lifecycle marketer
**Goals**
- Activate personalized recommendation content in email and campaign journeys.
- Target audiences by intent, purchase history, and seasonality.

**Pain points**
- Recommendation content is hard to generate consistently outside the ecommerce site.
- Campaign logic and recommendation logic are often disconnected.

**Behaviors**
- Plans sends around events, replenishment moments, and seasonal campaigns.
- Needs reusable recommendation sets and performance reporting.

**Success looks like**
- Emails and campaigns contain relevant looks that improve engagement and purchase.

### 6. Product / analytics / optimization lead
**Goals**
- Measure business impact, recommendation quality, and experiment outcomes.
- Prioritize improvements to data quality, ranking logic, and surface coverage.

**Pain points**
- Recommendation systems are often hard to evaluate end to end.
- Inconsistent events make attribution and experimentation unreliable.

**Behaviors**
- Tracks lift, attach rate, CTR, conversion, and override behavior.
- Uses experiments and telemetry to refine platform decisions.

**Success looks like**
- Recommendation logic is measurable, tunable, and trusted by stakeholders.
